अनुस्वारप्रकरण
The Dot That Changes Everything — three sūtra-s from Pāṇini that govern the humble bindu (ं)
GRAMMAR · DEEP DIVE
10 MIN READ
BEGINNER–INTERMEDIATE
येनाक्षरसमाम्नायमधिगम्य महेश्वरात् । कृत्स्नं व्याकरणं प्रोक्तं तस्मै पाणिनये नमः ॥ Salutations to Pāṇini, who, having learnt the alphabet from Maheśvara himself, expounded the complete science of grammar.
वाक्यकारं वररुचिं भाष्यकारं पतञ्जलिम् । पाणिनिं सूत्रकारं च प्रणतोऽस्मि मुनित्रयम् ॥ I bow to the triad of sages — Vararuci the author of the Vākyapādīya, Patañjali the author of the Mahābhāṣya, and Pāṇini the author of the Sūtra-s.

Sanskrit is often described as the most precisely constructed language in human history. That precision did not arrive by accident — it was engineered over millennia and codified by Pāṇini in his Ashtādhyāyī (अष्टाध्यायी), a grammar of roughly 4,000 sūtra-s composed around the 4th century BCE. Each sūtra is a surgical rule: minimal in words, maximum in scope.

Today we zoom into one small but consequential mark — the Anusvāra (अनुस्वार), also called the Bindu (बिन्दु, "drop"). Written as a dot above a letter (ं), it is a nasal resonance that appears constantly in Sanskrit text yet is often misunderstood. Three rules from the Ashtādhyāyī tell the complete story.

PREREQUISITES
Two Categories You Must Know

Before the rules make sense, Pāṇini's notation system requires two definitions — both are pratyāhāra-s, compressed labels for entire sets of sounds.

अच् ac
The collective name for all vowels (स्वर) in Sanskrit. अ आ इ ई उ ऊ ऋ ॠ ऌ ॡ ए ऐ ओ औ
हल् hal
The collective name for all consonants (व्यञ्जन) in Sanskrit. ह य व र ल ञ म ङ ण न झ भ घ ढ ध ज ब ग ड द ख फ छ ठ थ च ट त क प श ष स

The shorthand is elegant: when a grammarian writes ac, every vowel is implied; when they write hal, every consonant is implied. With this, a rule like "म् before hal" covers thirty-three consonants in two syllables.

SŪTRA 1 · ASHTĀDHYĀYĪ 8.3.23
When म् Becomes a Dot
RULE_01 mo'nusvāraḥ
मोऽनुस्वारः

When the consonant म् (m) is followed by any hal letter (any consonant), it transforms into an Anusvāra.

This is the entry point. Sanskrit words frequently end in म् — verbs, pronouns, nouns in the accusative case. Whenever such a word is followed by another word beginning with a consonant, the terminal म् sheds its hard closure and becomes the floating nasal resonance: ं.

RULE_01 · EXAMPLES
INPUT फलम् खादामि → फलं खादामि "I eat the fruit."
INPUT अहम् स्मरामि → अहं स्मरामि "I remember."
INPUT रामम् नमति → रामं नमति "He bows to Rāma."
COROLLARY A — म् before a vowel

When म् is followed by an ac (vowel), Rule 1 does not apply. The म् stays as-is, though sandhi may merge the sounds:

अहम् अस्मि → unchanged, or written together as अहमस्मि ("I am")
त्वम् इच्छसि → unchanged, or त्वमिच्छसि ("You desire")

COROLLARY B — म् at the end of an utterance

When म् ends a sentence or paragraph with nothing following it, there is no trigger — no transformation happens.

विघ्नेशोऽहम् — "I am Vighnesa" (stands alone, anusvāra does not apply)

"The dot is not decoration — it is a phonetic consequence of grammar in motion."
// ON THE ANUSVĀRA
SŪTRA 2 · ASHTĀDHYĀYĪ 8.4.58
The Dot Finds Its Family
RULE_02 anusvārasya yayi parasvarṇaḥ
अनुस्वारस्य ययि परसवर्णः

Applied after Rule 1. When an Anusvāra is followed by a vargīya vyañjana (a consonant belonging to one of the five primary rows), the Anusvāra is replaced by the nasal consonant of that row.

To understand this rule, we must understand Sanskrit's systematic consonant classification — the Varga system.

VARGA LETTERS (1–4) 5TH = NASAL PLACE OF ARTICULATION
क-वर्ग क ख ग घ ङ् Velar (throat)
च-वर्ग च छ ज झ ञ् Palatal
ट-वर्ग ट ठ ड ढ ण् Retroflex
त-वर्ग त थ द ध न् Dental
प-वर्ग प फ ब भ म् Labial (lips)

The fifth letter of each varga is a nasal. The rule's logic is phonetic harmony: when a nasal sound (the anusvāra) precedes a consonant, it assimilates to the same place of articulation as that consonant. Your mouth, in effect, starts preparing for the next sound.

THREE-STEP RESOLUTION
1
Identify the consonant that follows the anusvāra.
2
Identify which varga that consonant belongs to.
3
Replace the anusvāra with the nasal (5th letter) of that varga.
RULE_02 · EXAMPLES
दंत → त is त-वर्ग · nasal = न् → दन्त "tooth"
गंगा → ग is क-वर्ग · nasal = ङ् → गङ्गा "Ganges"
व्यंजन → ज is च-वर्ग · nasal = ञ् → व्यञ्जन "consonant"
घंटा → ट is ट-वर्ग · nasal = ण् → घण्टा "bell"
चंपा → प is प-वर्ग · nasal = म् → चम्पा "champak flower"

Watch the chain of rules interact on a full sentence:

अहम् पश्यामि  →  Rule 1 (म् before प्) →  अहं पश्यामि  →  Rule 2 (ं before प्, प-वर्ग nasal is म्) →  अहम् पश्यामि

It circles back to the original form — the grammar is demonstrating that the anusvāra in sandhi is phonetically equivalent to the original म् when the nasal resolves. Both written forms are valid.

COROLLARY — Non-vargīya consonants

Rule 2 applies only to the 25 vargīya consonants. The remaining consonants — र, श, ष, स, ह — have no corresponding nasal, so the anusvāra is unchanged before them.

सिंह remains सिंह (not *सिन्ह)  ·  वंश remains वंश (not *वन्श)

A special note on य, ल, व — while not vargīya, these do technically cause a change per a related sūtra. For practical purposes, however, forms like संयुक्त and संवाद are accepted as-is in standard usage.

SŪTRA 3 · ASHTĀDHYĀYĪ 8.4.59
Freedom at the Word's Edge
RULE_03 vā padāntasya
वा पदान्तस्य

When an Anusvāra stands at the end of a pada (word), the conversion to the nasal specified by Rule 2 is optional (vā = "either way").

Pāṇini is remarkably practical here. Rule 3 is really Rule 2's pressure valve — it acknowledges that at word boundaries, phonetic assimilation is desirable but not compulsory. Both the anusvāra form and the full-nasal form are grammatically correct.

RULE_03 · BOTH FORMS VALID
OPTION A अहं पश्यामि (anusvāra retained) ✓
OPTION B अहम् पश्यामि (nasal resolved) ✓
OPTION A रामं नमति (anusvāra retained) ✓
OPTION B रामन्नमति (nasal resolved, words merge) ✓

This optionality explains the stylistic variation you see across Sanskrit manuscripts, inscriptions, and modern printed texts — different editors and traditions have exercised this optionality differently. Neither is "wrong."

SUMMARY
Three Rules, One Dot
मोऽनुस्वारः म् + hal → anusvāra (mandatory)
ययि परसवर्णः anusvāra + vargīya → varga nasal (mandatory)
वा पदान्तस्य at word-end: nasal conversion is optional

The Anusvāra is not an aesthetic flourish. It is a live grammatical process — the aftermath of Rule 1, and the input to Rule 2. Understanding it unlocks why दन्त and दंत are both found in literature, why गङ्गा is the "classical" spelling, and why your grammar textbook writes व्यञ्जन the way it does.

Pāṇini, in roughly six syllables per sūtra, compressed a phonological system so precise that it would take linguists two millennia to fully appreciate. Three sūtra-s. One bindu. The dot that changes everything.
